The Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) has released Consumer Price Index (CPI) of goods in the Philippines with respect to 2018 prices. The following are the complete list of goods in the PSA report:

  • Food and Non-Alcoholic Beverages
  • Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco
  • Clothing and Footwear
  • Housing, Water, Electricity, Gas and Other Fuels
  • Furnishings, Household Equipment and Routine Household Maintenance
  • Health
  • Transport
  • Information and Communication
  • Recreation, Sport and Culture
  • Education Services
  • Restaurants and Accommodation Services
  • Financial Services
  • Personal Care, and Miscellaneous Goods and Services

CPI is a metric used to reveal inflation and cost of living. An increase in CPI is indicative to an increase in price relative to a certain period of time. Last June 2025, PSA reports that the CPI of common goods rose to 127% relative to 2018 prices. In this page, we will discuss which goods are mostly affected and which ones are not to identify which ones are more prone to inflation and those that are more resistant to it. We will be using Python as a tool help us cluster our data and have a meaningful analysis.

We then end up with the clustering chart above. We can identify four (4) unique clusters, namely: red, brown, purple, and grey.

  • The red cluster consists of basic necessities and consistent services like sports, recreation, water, and education. The red cluster usually falls between -2.5 to 0 PC1. These commodities are less volatile to price changes.
  • The brown cluster consists of goods more sensitive to external economic conditions like transportion, food, accomodation, and restaurants. It falls between +2.5 to +5 of PC1.
  • The purple cluster consists only of Financial Services. This might indicate that Financial Services are highly volatile to price changes and inflation. Since financial institutions depend on lending margins, credit demand, and investment returns, they are sensitive to interest rate changes, which are driven by CPI. Inflation could also reduce consumers' purchasing power which could be more riskier for financial institutions which could then lead to them increasing rates to mitigate possible losses.
  • The grey cluster consists only of Information and Communication. It has a PC1 of less than -7.5. This might indicate that inflation doesn't really trigger change in its prices. Internet and other communication means prices are relatively stable. Adding more context to their costs also helps understand why they're rarely affected by inflation. For simplicity, their fixed costs are high, but their variable costs are low. Towers, cables, and satellites are materials needed for their business and comes with high capital. However, they have low variable cost per user. Hence, need for price changes is rarely needed. It could also be the case that there could have been changes in the perception and need for internet. Previously, internet was seen as a luxury rather than a necessity, but with current subscription prices and it's demand inelasticity, this could point out that internat has now been a necessity for some or at least perceived as a necessity.

Overall, this study only shows commodities and goods that are more related to each other and how changes in CPI affect each cluster as a whole. A further study across all commodities per identified cluster would be great to further dig dive factors affecting each prices and consumer behavior.
