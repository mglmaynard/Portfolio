Rental Price Distribution Analysis in NCR and Region IV-A via Kernel Density Estimation

Introduction

This study focuses on the National Capital Region (NCR) and Region IV-A (Calabarzon) due to their significant economic contributions and high population densities within the Philippines. NCR contributes around 36% of the country’s GDP and is the most densely populated region. Region IV-A is the fastest-growing region and accounts for about 20% of the national GDP.

Both regions experience strong demand for rental housing driven by rapid urbanization and diverse housing needs, ranging from affordable units to high-end apartments. Understanding rental price distributions in these areas is crucial for informed housing policies and development strategies addressing the country’s most dynamic rental markets.

In this analysis, we examined the distribution of rental prices in NCR using grouped data categorized into price brackets. Since the data is reported in ranges (e.g., Php 1,001–Php 1,500), we represented each price bracket by its midpoint value to approximate the rental price for that category.

To analyze the distribution more accurately, we created a weighted dataset by repeating each midpoint value according to the number of renter households in that price bracket. For example, if 25,699 households fall within the Php 1,001–Php 1,500 bracket, the midpoint of Php 1,250 was repeated 25,699 times to simulate individual rental prices.

From this weighted dataset, we calculated two key statistical parameters: Mean (μ): The weighted average rental price, representing the central tendency of the data. Standard Deviation (σ): A measure of the dispersion or variability in rental prices around the mean, indicating how spread out the values are.

Using these parameters, we fitted a normal distribution curve to the data. The normal distribution is a well-known probability distribution characterized by its bell shape and is commonly used to model real-world continuous data. The curve's shape is determined by the mean and standard deviation of the dataset.

Plotting this curve alongside the histogram of actual rental prices allowed us to visually assess how closely the rental price data follows a normal distribution. This approach helps summarize the rental market, identify typical rental price ranges, and understand the variability in prices.

By modeling the data in this way, we gain insights that can support forecasting, decision-making, and further statistical analyses.

To assess whether the rental price data follows a normal distribution, we conducted two standard statistical tests: the Shapiro-Wilk test and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test. Both tests evaluate the hypothesis that the data is normally distributed.

Our analysis used weighted samples created by replicating rental price midpoints according to the number of households in each price bracket. Given the large sample size (over a million data points per region), the tests were sensitive to even small deviations from normality.

The results were as follows:

  • Both the Shapiro-Wilk and Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests yielded very low p-values (p < 0.001) for NCR and Region IV-A.
  • A low p-value indicates strong evidence to reject the assumption of normality in the rental price data.

This outcome suggests that while the data may appear somewhat bell-shaped visually, it does not perfectly conform to a normal distribution. This deviation is common in real-world data, especially when prices are grouped into brackets and influenced by market factors.

Recognizing these limitations, we next use a Kernel Density Estimate (KDE) to visualize the rental price distribution more flexibly, without assuming any particular distribution form.

Kernel Density Estimate

Likewise, let's add the result of Region IV-A

The rental price data available is reported in discrete price ranges (brackets) rather than exact continuous values. Each price range (e.g., Php 1,001–Php 1,500) groups households paying rent within that interval, which means the data is grouped and categorical in nature.

To perform a more detailed analysis, we approximated these brackets by their midpoint values (e.g., Php 1,250 for the Php 1,001–Php 1,500 range). By repeating these midpoint values according to the number of households in each bracket, we constructed a weighted dataset that simulates individual rental prices.

Using this weighted dataset, we applied a Kernel Density Estimate (KDE), a statistical tool that creates a smooth, continuous curve representing the distribution of rental prices. Although the original data is grouped, KDE helps approximate the underlying continuous distribution by smoothing the weighted midpoints.

This approach provides a more intuitive and detailed visualization of rental price patterns, revealing clusters and multiple common rent levels that would be difficult to see with grouped bar charts alone.

The Kernel Density Estimate (KDE) plots for both NCR and Region IV-A show several noticeable peaks in the rental price distributions. This means the data is multi-modal, where rental prices tend to cluster around multiple common values instead of just one average.

This happens because:

  • Different types of rental properties—such as affordable housing, mid-range apartments, and high-end units—attract renters with different budgets, creating distinct clusters.
  • Variations in household income, neighborhood characteristics, and housing quality lead to multiple groups of renters paying different rent amounts.
  • The original data is grouped in price brackets, which can highlight these clusters by how households are counted in each range.

Understanding these multiple peaks is important because it shows how diverse the rental market really is. Recognizing the existence of different renter groups helps landlords, policymakers, and developers create strategies tailored to each segment’s needs.

Conclusion

Analyzing the rental price data for NCR and Region IV-A reveals a complex and diverse rental market. The data does not perfectly follow a normal distribution, which is expected given the grouping of prices and market diversity. The Kernel Density Estimate (KDE) plots further highlight this by showing multiple peaks, indicating distinct renter groups across different price ranges.

This insight is valuable for understanding the rental landscape more deeply. It emphasizes the need for tailored approaches in housing policies, development planning, and market strategies that consider the varied segments within the rental market. By recognizing these patterns, stakeholders can better address the specific needs of renters and promote more effective housing solutions.
