Rental Price Distribution Analysis in NCR and Region IV-A via Kernel Density Estimation

Introduction

This study focuses on the National Capital Region (NCR) and Region IV-A (Calabarzon) due to their significant economic contributions and high population densities within the Philippines. NCR contributes around 36% of the country’s GDP and is the most densely populated region. Region IV-A is the fastest-growing region and accounts for about 20% of the national GDP.

Both regions experience strong demand for rental housing driven by rapid urbanization and diverse housing needs, ranging from affordable units to high-end apartments. Understanding rental price distributions in these areas is crucial for informed housing policies and development strategies addressing the country’s most dynamic rental markets.

In this analysis, we examined the distribution of rental prices in NCR using grouped data categorized into price brackets. Since the data is reported in ranges (e.g., Php 1,001–Php 1,500), we represented each price bracket by its midpoint value to approximate the rental price for that category.

To analyze the distribution more accurately, we created a weighted dataset by repeating each midpoint value according to the number of renter households in that price bracket. For example, if 25,699 households fall within the Php 1,001–Php 1,500 bracket, the midpoint of Php 1,250 was repeated 25,699 times to simulate individual rental prices.

From this weighted dataset, we calculated two key statistical parameters: Mean (μ): The average rental price, indicating the central tendency of the data. Standard Deviation (σ): The measure of spread or variability in rental prices around the mean.

Using these parameters, we fitted a normal distribution curve to the data. The normal distribution is a well-known probability distribution characterized by its bell shape and is commonly used to model real-world continuous data. The curve's shape is determined by the mean and standard deviation of the dataset.

Plotting this curve alongside the histogram of actual rental prices allowed us to visually assess how closely the rental price data follows a normal distribution. This approach helps summarize the rental market, identify typical rental price ranges, and understand the variability in prices.

By modeling the data in this way, we gain insights that can support forecasting, decision-making, and further statistical analyses.

We then end up with the clustering chart above. We can identify four (4) unique clusters, namely: red, brown, purple, and grey.

  • The red cluster consists of basic necessities and consistent services like sports, recreation, water, and education. The red cluster usually falls between -2.5 to 0 PC1. These commodities are less volatile to price changes.
  • The brown cluster consists of goods more sensitive to external economic conditions like transportion, food, accomodation, and restaurants. It falls between +2.5 to +5 of PC1.
  • The purple cluster consists only of Financial Services. This might indicate that Financial Services are highly volatile to price changes and inflation. Since financial institutions depend on lending margins, credit demand, and investment returns, they are sensitive to interest rate changes, which are driven by CPI. Inflation could also reduce consumers' purchasing power which could be more riskier for financial institutions which could then lead to them increasing rates to mitigate possible losses.
  • The grey cluster consists only of Information and Communication. It has a PC1 of less than -7.5. This might indicate that inflation doesn't really trigger change in its prices. Internet and other communication means prices are relatively stable. Adding more context to their costs also helps understand why they're rarely affected by inflation. For simplicity, their fixed costs are high, but their variable costs are low. Towers, cables, and satellites are materials needed for their business and comes with high capital. However, they have low variable cost per user. Hence, need for price changes is rarely needed. It could also be the case that there could have been changes in the perception and need for internet. Previously, internet was seen as a luxury rather than a necessity, but with current subscription prices and it's demand inelasticity, this could point out that internat has now been a necessity for some or at least perceived as a necessity.

Overall, this study only shows commodities and goods that are more related to each other and how changes in CPI affect each cluster as a whole. A further study across all commodities per identified cluster would be great to further dig dive factors affecting each prices and consumer behavior.
