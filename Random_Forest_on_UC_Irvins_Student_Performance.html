Introduction

In this project, I explore the application of Random Forest, a powerful ensemble machine learning algorithm, to predict student academic outcomes using the UCI Student Performance dataset. This dataset contains detailed records of students from Portuguese secondary schools, including demographic information, family background, study habits, and previous grades.

The goal is to develop a predictive model that classifies whether a student will pass or fail based on key factors such as study time, school absences, and family support. Random Forest is particularly well-suited for this task due to its ability to handle diverse types of data, model complex interactions, and reduce overfitting by aggregating predictions from multiple decision trees.

By leveraging this method, the project aims to identify the most influential variables affecting student success and demonstrate how data-driven insights can support educational decision-making and personalized interventions.

Random Forest Modeling

The Random Forest classifier was initialized with a fixed random seed for reproducibility. This ensures that the same set of trees is generated each time the model is run, which is essential when comparing results or sharing the analysis.

Although Random Forest builds many trees, the figure above shows a single decision tree as an example. Each split is based on a student feature such as grades, study time, or absences. The tree helps illustrate how the model makes pass/fail decisions, though in practice Random Forest relies on the aggregated predictions of hundreds of such trees for stability and accuracy.

The classification report indicates an overall accuracy of 87%. The precision, recall, and F1-scores are strong for both the "pass" (1) and "fail" (0) categories, with the model performing slightly better at identifying students who will pass. This demonstrates the effectiveness of Random Forest in handling imbalanced student outcomes.

Model Evaluation

The confusion matrix further confirms the classification performance. Out of 79 students, the model correctly predicted 23 out of 26 fails and 46 out of 53 passes. Only a small number of students were misclassified, which is acceptable in most educational prediction contexts.

Feature importance analysis reveals that the strongest predictors of academic success are the studentsâ€™ previous grades (G1 and G2). Other influential variables include absences and number of past failures. Social and demographic features such as school support or family support had less predictive power. This suggests that academic history outweighs external factors when predicting performance outcomes.

Conclusion

The Random Forest model achieved an accuracy of 87%, demonstrating strong predictive performance in classifying student outcomes. The classification report highlights high precision and recall for both pass and fail categories, while the confusion matrix confirms balanced predictive capability.

The feature importance analysis shows that previous grades (G1 and G2) are the most critical factors influencing student performance, followed by absences and number of failures. This aligns with expectations, as academic history is a strong predictor of future success.

These findings suggest that targeted interventions could be designed to support students with low prior grades, high absenteeism, or multiple past failures. By focusing resources on these areas, schools can take a more proactive approach in preventing academic failure and promoting student success.
